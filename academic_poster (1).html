From Document to Dialogue

Leveraging Teacher Awareness Sheets for Systemic Inclusion in Higher Education Assessment and Feedback

A Study by Lawrence Krukrubo, presented in pursuit of Fellowship with The Higher Education Academy (FHEA)

Methodology

This study employed a qualitative desktop research methodology, conducting a systematic analysis of contemporary factors, challenges, and opportunities in inclusive higher education. The research synthesized findings from a curated selection of high-quality, peer-reviewed academic papers published within the last 10 years. Key sources included scholarly articles on inclusive pedagogy, disability studies, and educational technology. The analysis was anchored by a detailed case study from the University of Wolverhampton on the use of Teacher Awareness Sheets (TAS), providing a practical lens through which to interpret the broader literature. The primary aim was to trace the evolution of support models, from reactive adjustments to proactive, systemic frameworks like Universal Design for Learning (UDL) and Assessment for Inclusion (AfI).

The Rising Tide of Disclosure

The rate of UK students disclosing a disability has nearly tripled in two decades, from 5.9% in 2003/4 to 15.7% in 2021/22.
This trend is a double-edged sword for the sector.

✔ A Success for Inclusion

  • Indicates a reduction in stigma surrounding disability.
  • Shows growing student confidence in institutional support systems.
  • Represents a key success for widening participation agendas.

⚠ A System Under Strain

  • Places immense administrative and pedagogical pressure on universities.
  • Exposes the limitations of the support infrastructure.
  • Creates a "scalability crisis" as reactive adjustment models prove unsustainable.

Key Findings

1. The "Static Document" Trap

The reliance on Tutor Awareness Sheets (TAS) as fixed, one-way documents is a primary barrier. Students report these plans often feel "generic" and fail to address specific contextual needs, placing the burden of advocacy on the individual.

The Flawed One-Way Process

2. Practitioner Anxiety is Systemic

Academic staff report high levels of anxiety and a lack of confidence in implementing adjustments. This is not due to a lack of willingness, but a systemic lack of mandatory, practical training in inclusive pedagogy and disability support.

The Weight of Uncertainty

3. The Power of Partnership

The most effective inclusive practices are dialogic. Case study evidence shows that when a TAS is used as the start of a conversation, leading to co-designed, contextual adjustments, student engagement and success are significantly enhanced.

The Dialogic Bridge

4. Technology's Dual Role

Digital tools offer powerful ways to create accessible learning environments, but this is compounded by a 'digital adjustment gap', where support depends on the compatibility of assistive tech with institutional platforms. Furthermore, the uncritical adoption of AI risks introducing algorithmic bias, as systems trained on majority data may treat students with disabilities as 'outliers', creating new forms of exclusion if not governed by robust ethical frameworks.

Discussion: The Chasm Between Policy and Practice

A significant chasm exists between the inclusive aspirations of university policies (underpinned by the Equality Act 2010) and the lived reality of students and staff. The current model, which treats a TAS as a static "boundary object," fails because it doesn't facilitate a shared understanding between disability services, academics, and students. This leads to a fragmented, inefficient, and unsustainable system that relies on the "heroics" of individual, highly-engaged tutors rather than reliable institutional processes. To bridge this chasm, the sector must shift its focus from perfecting the document to perfecting the process. The goal is not a better TAS, but a better conversation—one that is proactive, collaborative, and embeds the principles of advanced pedagogical frameworks like UDL and Assessment for Inclusion (AfI) into everyday practice.

Paradigm Shift: From Reactive Adjustments to Proactive Inclusion

Claims, Conclusions, and Vision

Claim: The prevailing model of using static support documents is fundamentally broken and unsustainable in the face of rising student disclosure rates.

Conclusion: Genuine inclusion cannot be achieved through bureaucratic compliance. It requires a systemic cultural shift towards a pedagogical model where support is dynamic, dialogic, and digitally-mediated. This involves empowering and training academics to become active partners in the support process.

Vision - The D3 Framework: We propose a future built on the D3 (Dynamic, Dialogic, Digitally-Mediated) Framework. In this vision, the TAS becomes a 'living document' on a central hub, mandating a structured conversation between student and tutor at the start of each module. This process uses technology not to replace human interaction, but to facilitate it, ensuring that support is always contextual, co-created, and effective. Adopting this framework is a strategic imperative to enhance student success, meet legal duties, and improve the quality of teaching and learning for all.
